# Pseudopotentials & PAW

> **Definition:** Methods that replace chemically inert core electrons and the nucleus with a smooth effective potential, reducing the number of explicitly treated electrons and the required kinetic energy cutoff. Norm-conserving pseudopotentials preserve the charge integral inside the core radius. Ultrasoft pseudopotentials relax this constraint, allowing lower cutoffs. The Projector Augmented Wave (PAW) method recovers full all-electron character near the nucleus via a linear transformation, combining USPP efficiency with all-electron accuracy. PAW is the default in VASP; NC-PP and USPP are standard in QE.

Core electrons don't care about chemistry. The 1s and 2s electrons of iron are the same in iron metal, iron oxide, and iron fluoride. So why recalculate them every SCF cycle? Pseudopotentials and PAW say: don't. Freeze the core, keep only the valence electrons, and replace the tightly oscillating all-electron wavefunctions near the nucleus with smooth, cheap-to-expand pseudowavefunctions.

---

## Hook

A plane-wave basis set for an all-electron calculation of iron would need `ENCUT` around 50,000 eV to resolve the core wavefunctions oscillating near the nucleus. With a PAW potential, you use 500 eV. That's two orders of magnitude fewer basis functions. This is not a minor optimization. It's what makes plane-wave DFT tractable for anything heavier than hydrogen.

---

## Why should you care?

The pseudopotential or PAW dataset you choose sets your energy cutoff, your accuracy near the nucleus, and your ability to compute properties that depend on the core region (hyperfine coupling, core-level shifts, magnetic moments at the nucleus). A wrong or mismatched potential introduces systematic errors that are invisible in a single calculation but corrupt energy differences.

---

## The wrong intuition

"Any pseudopotential for the right element gives the right answer."

Pseudopotentials are generated by fitting to a specific all-electron reference configuration using a specific functional. A PBE-generated PAW dataset and an LDA-generated one give different results. Mixing them between calculations invalidates energy differences. And some elements have multiple PAW datasets with different frozen core choices — Fe with semicore 3s and 3p treated as valence vs. Fe with only 3d and 4s as valence — which can affect magnetic moments significantly.

---

## The explanation

**The core radius problem.** Near a nucleus, the all-electron wavefunction oscillates rapidly (to remain orthogonal to the core states). These oscillations require high-frequency plane waves to represent — meaning a huge basis set. Beyond some cutoff radius $r_c$, the wavefunction is smooth and well-behaved. The oscillations only happen in the core region that doesn't change between chemical environments.

**Norm-conserving pseudopotentials (NC-PP).** Replace the all-electron potential and wavefunction inside $r_c$ with a smooth pseudo-wavefunction that matches the all-electron version outside $r_c$. The constraint is norm conservation: the integral of $|\psi_\text{ps}|^2$ inside $r_c$ equals that of $|\psi_\text{AE}|^2$. This ensures the correct charge in the core region and good transferability. Requires moderate cutoffs (60–100 Ry in QE for most elements).

**Ultrasoft pseudopotentials (USPP).** Relax the norm-conservation constraint. The pseudo-wavefunction is softer (smoother), requiring lower cutoffs. The missing charge is compensated by augmentation charges added analytically. Lower cutoff means smaller basis sets and faster calculations. Slightly less transferable than NC-PP but generally accurate for total energies and forces.

**Projector Augmented Wave (PAW).** The most rigorous approach. Instead of replacing the core region, PAW defines a linear transformation between a smooth pseudo-wavefunction and the true all-electron wavefunction. Inside each atom's augmentation sphere:

$$
|\psi\rangle = |\tilde{\psi}\rangle + \sum_i \left( |\phi_i\rangle - |\tilde{\phi}_i\rangle \right) \langle \tilde{p}_i | \tilde{\psi} \rangle
$$

$|\tilde{\psi}\rangle$ is the smooth pseudo part, $|\phi_i\rangle$ are the all-electron partial waves, $|\tilde{\phi}_i\rangle$ are the smooth partial waves, and $\langle \tilde{p}_i|$ are projectors. The transformation recovers full all-electron accuracy near the nucleus. VASP uses PAW by default; QE supports PAW but USPP and NC-PP are more common there.

**In practice,** you don't solve this equation yourself. You download a PAW dataset file (VASP's `POTCAR`, or QE's `.UPF` file) generated by someone who already did the all-electron fitting. Your job is to choose the right dataset for the right functional.

## Reality check

In QE, selecting a PAW dataset:

```
ATOMIC_SPECIES
  Fe  55.845  Fe.pbe-spn-kjpaw_psl.1.0.0.UPF
```

The filename tells you: `pbe` (functional), `spn` (s, p, n orbitals included in valence), `kjpaw` (PAW, Kresse-Joubert formulation), `psl` (PS library version).

In VASP, the `POTCAR` file is concatenated for all species in order. Always check `grep TITEL POTCAR` to confirm you have the right potentials in the right order.

Cutoff for a USPP in QE: `ecutwfc` sets the wavefunction cutoff, `ecutrho` sets the charge density cutoff. For USPP, `ecutrho` is typically 8-12× `ecutwfc`. For NC-PP, 4× is usually sufficient.

!!! warning "Common Mistake"
    Mixing pseudopotential libraries or DFT functional labels between codes without checking transferability. A VASP `POTCAR_PBE` and a QE `UPF_PBE` are not identical and will give slightly different energies even for the same functional in principle.

---

## Takeaway

The pseudopotential or PAW dataset is the link between your smooth plane-wave calculation and the real all-electron problem. Match the functional, use the same library throughout a comparison, and check what's included in the valence shell for heavy elements.
